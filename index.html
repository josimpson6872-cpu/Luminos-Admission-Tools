› Pain ★
Every Patient Requires a Pain Assessment

Use the following rules to determine how to document pain for each patient:

  • If pain is identified as a present issue — even if the patient's pain is currently well-managed with medications — pain MUST be included on the plan of care and a comprehensive pain assessment must be completed.
  • Pain intensity level can be zero (0) if at the time of assessment their pain is well palliated. The nurse should assess the characteristics of the pain when the patient is experiencing it and document accordingly.
  • If the patient truly has no issue with pain and is not taking any pain medications, then pain can be marked as not a problem.
📝
PRN "Just in Case" Medications: A PRN medication ordered as a precaution — such as morphine from the Ekit available for potential future pain — does NOT trigger a comprehensive pain assessment requirement if it is not actively being used. Only medications the patient is currently taking for pain determine whether pain is an active problem.
Comprehensive Pain Assessment

If a patient is identified as having pain, a Comprehensive Pain Assessment is required. At least 5 of the 7 indicators must be assessed for it to count as a comprehensive pain assessment:

ℹ
Attempted assessments count: As long as a component was attempted — even if unable to be fully completed — it counts toward the 5 of 7 requirement. Document what was attempted, the method used, and the reason it could not be fully assessed. Leaving a component completely blank without documentation of the attempt is not acceptable.

Component (Firenote Field)What to Assess
Location (Primary Pain Location)Where is the pain? All sites; localized or diffuse.
Severity (Pain Intensity)0–10 scale. Non-verbal: use FLACC or PAINAD — document tool name and score.
Character (Verbal Pain Description)Aching, burning, stabbing, throbbing — use the patient's own words.
Duration (Pain Duration)Constant vs. intermittent; onset date or timeframe.
What Relieves (Pain Relieved By)Position, activity, medications, heat/cold — what makes it better?
What Worsens (Pain Increased By)Activity, position, movement, stress — what makes it worse?
Effect on Function / QOL (Pain Side Effects)Impact on mobility, sleep, appetite, mood, ADLs, social interaction.
📝
Firenote does not have a dedicated "Frequency" field. Document frequency in the Verbal Pain Description or narrative free-text.
📝
Pain Intensity Goal: Document what the patient identifies as an acceptable pain intensity level.
Pain Intensity Warranting Further Action: Document at what point the patient wants to pursue additional measures for more aggressive pain control (e.g., increased medication dosing that may cause side effects such as increased somnolence).
ℹ
Non-verbal/unresponsive patients: A patient does NOT need to be verbally responsive for a pain assessment to be documented. Document clinical observation, caregiver input, behavioral cues, and the validated tool used. Leaving the assessment blank because the patient could not respond is not acceptable.

Disease-Specific LCDs

Complete the General and Decline LCD sections on every patient. Then complete only the one disease-specific LCD that applies to your patient's primary hospice diagnosis. Do not complete all LCDs — only the one that matches the primary diagnosis.

LCD SectionKey Documentation Points
Alzheimer's / DementiaThe FAST scale was designed for Alzheimer's Disease and is most validated in that context. Luminos policy supports its use for other dementia types as well — if used for non-Alzheimer's dementia, ensure your documentation goes beyond the scale score and clearly describes the patient's overall functional decline to support eligibility.
ALSDocument objective progression (respiratory decline, dysphagia, PPS changes). Avoid vague statements like "declining" — include measurable changes.
Heart DiseaseMust document NYHA Class IV symptoms or EF < 20% with supporting symptoms. Clearly indicate symptoms at rest and lack of response to treatment.
CancerSpecify disease status (metastatic, locally advanced, progressive). Include PPS score and evidence of decline (weight loss, decreased intake, treatment tolerance).
HIVInclude most recent CD4 count and viral load if available. Also document opportunistic infections and functional decline.
Liver DiseaseInclude MELD score if available. Must document at least one major complication (ascites, encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome, SBP).
Pulmonary DiseaseDocument objective measures (FEV1 if available, O2 dependence). Must include dyspnea at rest and declining functional status.
Renal DiseaseDocument GFR/creatinine values and clearly state that dialysis is not being pursued or has been discontinued.
StrokeUse sequelae codes (I69.x) rather than acute CVA codes when appropriate. Document functional deficits, PPS, and impact on intake/swallowing.
ComaDocument cause (if known) and confirm lack of improvement. Include neurologic status and duration (≥ day 3 if applicable).

Drug Profile Review

A comprehensive drug profile review is required on all new admissions, with medication changes, and with each recertification. This review requires the nurse to assess:

  • Medication appropriateness
  • Potential drug-to-drug interactions
  • Potential drug allergies
  • The need for any laboratory monitoring (e.g., INR monitoring for patients on Coumadin)
  • Appropriate medications to consider for deprescribing that are no longer medically necessary
Review ItemWhat to Do
Care Plan Med ReviewReview the medications listed on the Care Plan and mark each one as Reviewed to document you've reviewed current meds during the visit.

Reminder: Identify and document who is responsible for medication administration. Provide education on each medication including purpose, dosing, administration, and potential side effects. Confirm patient/caregiver verbalized understanding.
Med Interaction ReviewCheck for potential interactions. Drug-to-drug interactions and drug allergy items will populate in a red box above the med list. The admission nurse MUST notate each specifically, discuss with the hospice physician, and document the outcome.

If drug-to-drug interactions are identified, add the following care plan problem:

Problem: Z91.89 — Other Personal Risk Factors, Not Elsewhere Classified
Goal: Patient will remain free from adverse medication effects as measured by absence of observed signs/symptoms of medication-related complications, safe and appropriate medication regimen, and ongoing monitoring and timely intervention for identified risks by end of the current benefit period.

Interventions:
• Review medication profile for: Drug-to-drug interactions | Duplicate therapies | Allergy risks
• Reconcile medications at each visit and with any medication change
• Educate patient/caregiver on: Purpose of medications, potential side effects and interactions, and when to report symptoms
• For each interaction identified — copy and paste from the red box in the med list:
  Alert physician of: [Interaction name and medications]
  Monitor for: [Interaction details and clinical risk]

Important: Document physician reply in the comment field when received. Once physician has responded, the "Alert physician" intervention may be discontinued — keep "Monitor for" active. Do NOT discontinue "Monitor for" unless the interaction alert disappears because the medication was discontinued.
Duplicate Med ReviewIdentify and remove duplicate medication entries (often patient-reported meds that duplicate covered or encounter-ordered meds) to avoid confusion.

Care Plan

Build the initial care plan for the patient
▼
!
Marking an intervention "Performed" is what populates Skilled Nursing Performed — one of the most commonly cited audit deficiencies. Do NOT close the visit until every performed intervention pertaining to this visit is checked.

The admitting nurse is responsible for building the initial care plan. At minimum, the care plan must address:

  • The Primary Hospice Diagnosis
  • Any comorbid conditions the patient has that are being monitored, managed, or reported on by the hospice
  • Any active symptoms being treated, educated on, or affecting comfort and function
  • Any QAPI-related events such as falls, infections, wounds, etc.
Care Plan Diagnosis / Problem List
  • All diagnoses — related AND unrelated — must be added to the active problem list. These do NOT all require full care plans built out, but they must be listed.
  • Unrelated diagnoses must be marked "Not Related" if they are truly not related to the overall prognosis and decline of the patient's condition.
  • Medications (scheduled and PRN) may be linked to a problem without requiring a separate goal per medication.

Not every diagnosis needs its own separate intervention set if it is clinically related to an existing problem.

Nursing Narrative

Document the patient's full clinical story
▼
!
Depression Screening must be documented manually in the nursing narrative — Firenote has no dedicated field. It must be entered manually on every admission.

Document a clear, detailed admission narrative describing the patient's current condition and overall decline. This is used by the physician to support the Attestation statement.

Required Narrative Elements
  • Primary diagnosis and related conditions
  • Secondary non-related conditions (comorbidities impacting overall status)
  • Evidence of decline (functional, nutritional, cognitive)
  • Objective data (PPS, weight/MAC changes, intake, ADLs)
  • Symptom burden (pain, dyspnea, anxiety, etc.)
  • Current functional status and level of dependence
  • Any recent changes, hospitalizations, or infections
  • Overall clinical picture supporting hospice eligibility
  • Confirmation that hospice-covered drugs, DME, and services have been reviewed with the patient/family
EXAMPLE — Hospice-Covered Drugs, DME & Services Review

Hospice-covered medications, durable medical equipment, and services were reviewed with [patient/family/caregiver] at the time of admission. Patient/caregiver verbalized understanding of what is covered under the hospice benefit and what may require separate authorization. Questions were addressed and answered to satisfaction.

    💡
    This narrative should tell the full story of the patient — both what is driving hospice eligibility and what contributes to their overall condition.
